Breast Cancer Diagnosis Features

Clump Thickness: Refers to the thickness of cell clusters. Higher values may indicate the formation of cell clumps, which can help detect signs of cancer.

Uniformity of Cell Size: Cancerous cells often vary in size. This feature measures how uniform the sizes of the cells are, aiding in identifying possible malignancies.

Uniformity of Cell Shape: Similar to cell size, cancerous cells often vary in shape. This feature assesses the consistency in the shape of the cells, assisting in the detection of abnormal growth.

Marginal Adhesion: In healthy tissue, cells stick to each other. A decrease in adhesion may be a sign of malignant tissue, where cells lose their normal adhesion properties.

Single Epithelial Cell Size: Measures the size of individual epithelial cells, which tend to be larger when signs of cancer are present.

Bare Nuclei: Refers to the presence of nuclei without the surrounding cytoplasm. Bare nuclei are more commonly found in malignant tumors, helping to distinguish between benign and malignant cases.

Bland Chromatin: Chromatin is the material that makes up chromosomes. In cancerous cells, chromatin can appear less structured, helping detect abnormalities.

Normal Nucleoli: Nucleoli are small, dense structures within the nucleus. Larger or more numerous nucleoli are often associated with malignancies and help detect rapid cell growth.

Mitoses: Refers to cell division. A higher number of mitoses is usually indicative of rapid growth, a common sign of cancer.

Breast Cancer Stages

Stage 0: This stage is called "in situ" or localized. Cancer cells are present but have not spread to nearby tissues. It's highly treatable.

Stage 1: The tumor is small (less than 2 cm), and cancer has not spread to lymph nodes or other organs.

Stage 2: The tumor is larger (2 to 5 cm) and may have spread to nearby lymph nodes but has not reached other parts of the body.

Stage 3: Cancer has spread more extensively to nearby tissues and lymph nodes. The tumor may be larger than 5 cm, and the cancer may be locally advanced.

Stage 4: The cancer has spread to distant parts of the body, such as bones, lungs, or liver. It is now referred to as metastatic cancer.

Stage 5: This is an advanced stage where the cancer has extensively spread throughout the body, potentially affecting multiple organs and systems, making treatment more complex and often palliative.
